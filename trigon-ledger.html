Three depths

Choose how far the test should go.

Light, Medium, and Heavy describe the depth of the run. They do not rank the importance of an idea, and they do not turn a result into proof.

△Light TestA clean first pass for overlap, clarity, and obvious failure points.
  • Scan the public archive for related work.
  • Define the question and framework parts.
  • Run a small scenario set.
  • Record ambiguity, nulls, and AI-added meaning.

Output: a reviewable first-pass record, without a validation claim.

△Medium TestA structured run with sources, controls, cross-checks, and reusable data.
  • Complete the archive scan and geometry preflight.
  • Lock variables, sources, and test conditions.
  • Add baseline, null, and negative controls.
  • Publish downloadable data when release rights allow it.

Output: a governed test package ready for deeper review or repair.

△Heavy TestA full research lane for preregistration, data lock, stress tests, and reproducibility.
  • Preregister the hypothesis, gates, and failure criteria.
  • Freeze data and analysis choices before outcome inspection.
  • Run robustness, sensitivity, provenance, and replication checks.
  • Publish code, data, checksums, and Record-P when cleared.

Output: the deepest public record the available evidence can honestly support.

Curiosity layer

Open any record to explore its source trail, methods, controls, evidence, AI-added scaffolding, ambiguity notes, failure history, and version history.

Sorting map

Records can be sorted by test depth, status, domain, update date, title, and eventually by source, method family, record type, and relationship to earlier work.

Public release gate

Only approved public material is indexed. Private Drive links, unreleased research, personal information, and restricted source files stay outside the public manifest.

Truth boundary: exploratory, failed, null, ambiguous, blocked, and superseded records remain visibly labelled.
Expandable by design

Every section can open deeper.

The first screen answers the basic question. Tapping or clicking reveals the evidence without forcing every visitor through a wall of technical detail.

Summary and claim ceiling

Plain-language question, result, current status, and the strongest interpretation the evidence permits.

Sources and provenance

Source IDs, creators, links, access dates, verification state, limitations, and the chain from source to record.

Methods and controls

Variables, preprocessing, equations, baselines, null tests, failure criteria, and known method limits.

Evidence and downloads

Released tables, data, code, figures, summaries, checksums, and file-level rights notes.

Ambiguity and failure history

What stayed unclear, what failed, what became non-reproducible, and which questions remain open.

AI-added scaffolding

Definitions, categories, confidence scales, interpretations, or structure introduced by an AI rather than supplied by the original framework.

Version and relationship history

Superseded versions, repairs, reproductions, replications, extensions, and links to related records.

Report a problem

A future feedback route should let readers flag broken links, calculation errors, privacy concerns, unclear claims, and provenance gaps without silently altering the record.

What the Ledger will never pretend.

  • A clean interface does not make a result correct.
  • A Heavy Test does not automatically outrank a careful Light Test.
  • An exploratory result does not become confirmation because it looks promising.
  • A download is useful only when its source, rights, units, method, and version are clear.
  • AI can help scan and organize the archive, but it does not become the judge of the evidence.
